Since 2009, most human settlements have shifted from pursuing individual inscriptions on the WH List toward collective efforts, cooperating with other settlements in the preparation of serial nominations and cultural landscapes [1]. This shift has led to an exponential growth in the number of settlements managing WH properties [2] and, with accelerating urban expansion, has introduced mounting challenges to WH management — from integrating heritage considerations into mainstream urban planning systems to withstanding the pressures imposed by conflicts and external threats [3], [4], [5], [6]. In response, the 2011 UNESCO Recommendation on the Historic Urban Landscape (HUL) called for a reorientation of WH management: from the protection of individual assets to the stewardship of their broader urban contexts [7]. Understanding World Heritage Cities (WHCs) and the diversity of forms they take is therefore a necessary foundation for this broader agenda.The combination of "World Heritage" and "cities" is often found used in practice and theory yet seldom accompanied by a definition [2], in part because no such definition directly appears in UNESCO documents [8]. Three existing pathways for classification each reveal their own limitations. UNESCO's Operational Guidelines historically classified city-related heritage under "historic towns and town centres", subdivided by state of use [9]. This typology was removed entirely in the 2021 revision in favor of a more flexible, attribute-based process [10], leaving no stable categorical framework in its place. The Organization of World Heritage Cities (OWHC), operating a membership network since 1993, relies on voluntary application, producing a list that is neither exhaustive nor standardized [11]. Other early academic work classified WH properties into two categories: those that are cities or towns themselves, and those that are sites situated within an urban context [12], [13]. In this classification, the research object remained the WH property rather than the city. None of these pathways yields a definition that is both inclusive of settlement diversity and analytically tractable at scale.Building on these limitations, this PhD research adopts the definition proposed by Roders, which takes cities directly as the research object and grounds classification in the spatial distribution of Outstanding Universal Value (OUV) [2]. WHCs are defined as all settlements that host cultural heritage properties inscribed on the UNESCO WH List, provided these properties are located within or at the outskirts of their urban areas. These settlements may vary in municipal classification — cities, towns, villages, or communes — and the properties themselves can range from a single building to all properties composing several entire settlements. As shown in  REF _Ref229607909 \h  \* MERGEFORMAT Figure 1, this definition yields four main categories defined by the spatial extent to which the WH property occupies the settlement: singular (a single monument), fragmented (dispersed buildings or building groups), concentrated (a defined historic district), and absolute (the entire settlement). Each category is further divided into individual and collective sub-types, depending on whether one or multiple human settlements jointly manage the WH property. For example, Melbourne (Australia) represents an individual settlement with singular OUV, as its inscription covers a single monument. Haifa (Israel) exemplifies the pattern of collective settlements with fragmented OUV, being one of three human settlements in a nomination of a group of dispersed buildings. Rabat (Morocco) illustrates an individual settlement with concentrated OUV, where the WH property corresponds to a defined historic urban district. However, since 2009 the rise of collective inscription has substantially increased the number of WHCs whose OUV is distributed across several settlements rather than concentrated within one [1], [14]. This shift may have introduced new typological patterns beyond the existing four-by-two framework, and altered the composition of cities within each existing pattern. Whether the current WH List is accurately reflected by this definition and its embedded classification thus remains an open question.When examining the WH with urban context, it is necessary to zoom out from in-depth case studies and establish a macro-level perspective. Existing bibliometric analyses indicate that there have been a large number of case studies focusing on context-specific issues, such as digital conservation, urban regeneration, risk assessment, and stakeholder coordination in particular cities [15], [16]. However, research at a zoom-out remains scarce, which limits the generalizability and scalability of findings. This gap is becoming more pressing: the rise of collective inscription since 2009 signals that WH management is evolving from a site-by-site model toward a networked one, and the socio-economic interdependencies have transformed what were once one-way relationships into complex urban networks [17], [18]. Capturing this networked complexity requires not merely more case studies, but a different kind of research design — one capable of cross-scale and multi-site comparison.The contribution of “zooming out” depends on how it is realized. One path prioritizes expanding the number of cities studied, pursuing breadth of coverage at the cost of analytical depth. Another path does not treat city count as an end in itself; it first establishes a globally covering data infrastructure, upon which substantive research can be conducted. The generalizability is grounded in this global structure, rather than accumulated through the number of cities examined. This PhD research pursues the latter: its core contribution lies not in repeating analyses across an ever-larger set of cities, but in constructing a shared and scalable global knowledge infrastructure from which sub-studies of different research focus can grow.Humans are an essential component of the urban context. Examining the interplay between human and different characteristics of the physical environment involves a multi-level, multi-disciplinary, and social-ecological approach, thus evolving a knowledge base for urban planning decisions [19], [20]. From a long-term and cyclical perspective, in the "people-to-place" sensing pathway, people act as "sensors" to provide "post-occupancy" feedback for urban development [21], [22], [23], [24], [25]. Human responses to the built environment offer a ground-level, experience-based lens on the urban qualities, which are particularly relevant where heritage significance is at stake. Although multiple senses influence human experience in urban settings, vision dominates the sensory prioritization hierarchy, accounting for approximately 70% of environmental perception [26]. This primacy of vision means that human responses to the urban environment are largely mediated through its design attributes, such as form, layout, and function. Accordingly, this PhD research focuses on these qualities of the urban built environment, while other dimensions fall outside its scope. Such human responses constitute a bottom-up source of environmental knowledge, which is grounded in lived experience rather than expert judgement.Data-driven methods offer a bridge between these two imperatives: they are well-suited to capturing human responses to the urban built environment, while also enabling the multi-city analysis that a zoomed-out perspective demands. Around 2010, the rise of big data in geographic information science introduced a wave of data-driven frameworks for urban studies [21], [27], [28], [29]. This led to a transition from conventional, manually collected data to more sophisticated, automated, and diverse data [30]. As outlined in the preliminary literature review of this PhD research, these data types are highly varied. Data sources include, but are not limited to, geographic information systems, automatic tracking, online visual media, and user-generated data (see the Appendix -to be updated for detailed branches and references). These sources directly constitute, or serve as experimental materials to generate, a large volume of bottom-up information. Among these, for unstructured data (text, images, etc.), emerging methods from data science and artificial intelligence (AI) enable large-scale analysis of their spatiotemporal patterns and semantic content. Several existing studies have established paradigmatic frameworks that have been widely applied by subsequent research. These frameworks utilize techniques of natural language processing [31], [32] and computer vision [33], [34] to interpret urban experiences from a human-centered perspective. Beyond these foundational frameworks, a wide range of more specialized methods have emerged in topic-specific multi-city studies. These include Siamese network models for detecting physical change in the built environment over time [35], multi-task deep learning for estimating ground-level vision from satellite imagery [36], concept bottleneck architectures for interpretable perceptual prediction [37], and graph kernel methods for quantifying morphological similarity between buildings [38]. Each carries potential for transferability beyond its original research context. However, a systematic review dedicated to the methodologies applied in multi-city studies remains absent. Existing reviews (see the Appendix -to be updated for details) do not go beyond bibliometric accounting and application classification, leaving it unclear what methods and datasets are available and which among them offer the best-suited scalability and transferability.The purpose of learning different data-driven methods from urban studies is to combine them with heritage-specific data for application in heritage studies. Bai et al. demonstrated one such dataset creation framework, which integrated social media text, images, and metadata with heritage values and heritage attributes to model spatiotemporal and social correlations, and then validated with several typical cities [39]. Although this analysis framework is predictably transferable to other cities, upscaling it to hundreds or even thousands of WHCs may present an entirely different challenge. While several data platforms (e.g. Protected Planet, World Heritage Outlook, and ResCult) have compiled spatial data on WH properties, they share critical limitations: most provide no more than point-level location data, spatial coverage is either restricted to specific regions or unevenly digitized, and the datasets lack a common structure to enable cross-city analysis [8]. A modular dataset covering WHCs globally, with consistent spatial referencing and extensible data interfaces, has yet to be constructed.Together, these challenges — methodological, definitional, and infrastructural — point to the need for methods-oriented research to understand WHCs at scale, one whose significance extends beyond the technical to the social and institutional.Social relevanceThe WH List represents the most systematic international attempt to identify and protect cultural and natural heritage of OUV, and as such it constitutes an indispensable reference for the comparative study of urban heritage globally. Yet the coverage of WH List is structurally constrained by the nomination mechanism through which it is built. Inscription on the WH List is a top-down process, which is shaped not only by the heritage significance of candidate properties, but also by the administrative capacity, financial resources, and diplomatic positioning of nominating states. These factors have been shown empirically to influence both inscription decisions and upgrades from negative advisory-body recommendations [40], [41]. The cumulative effect of these constraints is formally acknowledged in UNESCO's own evaluations: more than a quarter-century after the launch of the 1994 Global Strategy for a Representative, Balanced and Credible World Heritage List, its representational goals remain unmet in both geographic and typological terms, with European over-representation growing rather than shrinking [42], [43]. The WH List is therefore best understood not as a complete map of the world's urban heritage, but as a significant yet incomplete baseline. This PhD research takes it as the point of departure while investigating the heritage potential that lies beyond its current boundaries.Beyond the WH List lies a broader urban heritage landscape that current practice is ill-equipped to see. Veldpaus et al. established urban heritage as a systemic property of all cities rather than an attribute exclusive to listed ones [44]. This concept implies that heritage identification and management are relevant beyond the formal nomination process. Yet the implementation of this principle has remained concentrated on already-inscribed cities, with systematic application to unlisted urban contexts underrepresented in both research and practice [14], [45], [46]. This blind spot takes two concrete forms, both rooted in the top-down, expert-led nomination process. The first concerns verification: for urban areas whose municipalities have self-declared heritage potential by submitting properties to UNESCO's Tentative List, such claims currently lack independent validation beyond the nominating state's own assessment. The second concerns discovery: for urban areas with neither inscribed nor tentative WH properties, heritage value that exists outside the formal nomination process remains entirely undetected. The two layers of blind spot call for engaging knowledge from a wider range of stakeholders beyond official channels. This aligns with the HUL, which suggests that the approach of cultural heritage management should involve stakeholders from local to international, from private to public, and from experts to non-specialist participants. HUL also encourages the development of civic engagement and knowledge tools that help a diverse range of stakeholders to identify key values in their urban areas, as well as to monitor and manage changes in cultural significance and diversity [7]. In practice, however, a bottom-up perspective on heritage potential verification and discovery (HPVD) in urban areas remains unevenly implemented and rarely systematically assessed, particularly outside the context of already-inscribed properties [47], [48]. Addressing the heritage potential challenge requires two prerequisites. The first is a systematic exploration of the global knowledge within the current WH List, which is accumulated by decades of expert evaluation on OUV. Without a systematic characterization of inscribed cities in typological terms, the attempts to recognize comparable potential in unlisted cities may lack an operational point of reference. Building a globally covering, cross-city knowledge base from the WH List is therefore both an end in its own right and a foundation for the broader ambition of future research [49]. The second is a broader conception of whose knowledge counts in HPVD. Top-down nomination processes alone cannot capture the full range of cultural significance present in urban environments, and a bottom-up perspective — one grounded in the lived experience of urban inhabitants and the engagement of diverse local stakeholders — is equally indispensable.These two prerequisites resonate directly with the policy frameworks that have reshaped the international heritage agenda over the past decade, as summarized in  REF _Ref229827847 \h Table 1.Table  SEQ Table \* ARABIC 1 Policy frameworks/opinions motivating this PhD researchPolicy framework/opinionsCore claimCorrespondence with this researchReferencePolicy for the Integration of a Sustainable Development Perspective into the Processes of the World Heritage ConventionWorld Heritage Convention and Sustainable DevelopmentHeritage and the Sustainable Development Goals: Policy Guidance for Heritage and Development ActorsRethinking Heritage for Sustainable DevelopmentWH Convention & SDG alignment: WH management must align with SDGs; State-of-art and gapsThe preceding state-of-art reveals four interconnected gaps that collectively constrain the data-driven understanding of WHCs. To begin with, while data-driven methods for analyzing human responses to the urban built environment have proliferated, no systematic review has focused on methods at multi-city scale, making methodological choices difficult for large-scale research design. In parallel, the typological framework that defines and classifies WHCs needs to be updated, since the rise of collective inscription has changed the common heritage management approaches, leaving open the question of whether the patterns of WHCs are accurately captured. Building on both, the data infrastructure required for global cross-city analysis of WHCs does not yet exist, as existing platforms offer fragmented, non-standardized, and largely point-level spatial data, insufficient for systematic comparison. Finally, the current heritage recognition system relies heavily on top-down nomination processes, which creates two compounding blind spots: the self-declared heritage claims of cities on UNESCO's Tentative List lack independent empirical verification, and in regions underrepresented on the WH List, cities with unrecognized heritage potential remain invisible to the system entirely. Together, they point to an overarching gap: the absence of a scalable, data-driven framework that integrates methodological rigor, empirical grounding, and cross-city comparability for the global study of WHCs. Addressing this gap is the driving motivation of this PhD research. Research objectives and questionsThe overall aim of this PhD research is to develop a scalable, data-driven framework for understanding WHCs at the global scale, from the typological patterns of inscribed properties to the heritage potential of cities beyond the WH List.
